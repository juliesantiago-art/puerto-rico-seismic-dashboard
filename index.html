How the land is moving

≈ 1.5–2 cm / year

All of Puerto Rico is drifting toward the NE — as one nearly-rigid block — at about the speed your fingernails grow. The green arrows are real GPS stations; each shows which way and how fast the ground there is actually creeping.

It rides the Caribbean plate, grinding past the North American plate along the Puerto Rico Trench to the north. That motion is locked up on the faults — strain builds for decades, then releases as an earthquake. To the west, the Mona Passage is slowly pulling apart (the 1918 source).

What the magnitude means

The scale is logarithmic — each whole step is ~10× larger seismic-wave amplitude and about 32× more energy. So an M7 isn't “a bit worse” than M6, it releases ~32× the energy.

M5 felt widely, minor damage
M6 the 2020 Guánica M6.4 — serious local damage
M7 the 1918 Mona Passage M7.1 — deadly + tsunami
~M8 the 1787 event — the island's largest known (estimated from historical accounts)

Tsunami safety act fast

Puerto Rico's deadliest seismic threat is a near-field tsunami. In 1918 waves hit the west coast in minutes — there may be no time for an official warning. The shaking is your warning.

If you're near the coast and feel strong or long shaking: don't wait — move as high and as far inland as you can (at least ~30 m / 100 ft elevation or 2 km inland) immediately, and stay until officials say it's safe.

What can actually be done?

Earthquakes can't be predicted, prevented, or delayed with today's science — no method exists to stop or time a quake. What genuinely protects people:

  • Early warning — seconds of alert after a rupture starts.
  • Tsunami readiness — PR's west coast can be hit in minutes (1918). Know your evacuation route to high ground.
  • Building codes & retrofits — most quake deaths are from buildings, not shaking itself.
  • Drills & supplies — drop, cover, hold on; water, food, meds for days.
