How the land is moving

≈ 2 cm / year

All of Puerto Rico is drifting toward the ENE — as one nearly-rigid block — at about the speed your fingernails grow. The green arrows are GPS stations; each shows which way and how fast the ground there is creeping.

It rides the Caribbean plate, grinding past the North American plate along the Puerto Rico Trench to the north. That motion is locked up on the faults — strain builds for decades, then releases as an earthquake. To the west, the Mona Passage is slowly pulling apart (the 1918 source).

What can actually be done?

Earthquakes can't be predicted, prevented, or delayed with today's science — no method exists to stop or time a quake. What genuinely protects people:

  • Early warning — seconds of alert after a rupture starts.
  • Tsunami readiness — PR's west coast can be hit in minutes (1918). Know your evacuation route to high ground.
  • Building codes & retrofits — most quake deaths are from buildings, not shaking itself.
  • Drills & supplies — drop, cover, hold on; water, food, meds for days.

This tool helps you understand hazard and watch the land move — it is not an alarm system. For official alerts: Red Sísmica de PR & NOAA Tsunami Warning.

Sources: USGS ComCat · Red Sísmica de Puerto Rico (UPRM) · PB2002 plate model · GEM active faults · Nevada Geodetic Lab GPS · NOAA Caribbean Tsunami Warning Program.

How to read this map

A live look at where earthquakes happen around Puerto Rico, how the land is slowly moving, and what the long-term hazard looks like — built from authoritative open data (USGS, GEM, NGL).

The dots are earthquakes. Color = how deep (red = shallow → teal = deep). Bigger dot = stronger quake. The dense red cluster in the southwest is the 2020 Guánica sequence.

Green arrows = the land moving. GPS stations show the crust creeping ~2 cm/year as the plates grind — this is the strain that eventually releases as quakes.
